How to fact-check
what AI tells you

AI tools can be useful, but they also make things up with complete confidence. This guide will help you check them so you don't get burned.

6 steps · ~5 min read · For all experience levels

1
Understand why AI makes things up
It's not lying. It's predicting.

AI language models don't retrieve facts from a database. They predict the most statistically likely next word based on patterns from their training data, which means they can produce text that sounds authoritative and specific while being entirely fabricated. The technical term for this is "hallucination," but the practical effect is the same: a confident-sounding answer with nothing behind it.

AI output — looks credible, but fabricated

"According to a 2022 JAMA study by Dr. Elena Soto et al., patients receiving drug X showed a 34% reduction in symptom severity over 12 weeks."

What's actually going on

That study may not exist at all. The author name, journal, year, and statistic can all be invented and presented with complete confidence. A search on PubMed would turn up nothing.

Worth remembering: the more specific a claim sounds (names, dates, statistics, citations), the more it's worth checking carefully.
2
Know which claims need verification
Not everything AI says requires a deep fact-check

If you needed to fact-check everything AI produced, you might as well research and write from scratch. A more practical approach is to focus your energy on the types of claims that are more likely to be wrong:

Statistics and numbersPercentages, study results, market figures — specific enough to sound real, but are easy to fabricate
Named citationsAuthor names, journal titles, publication dates — these are often partially or fully invented
Recent eventsAI has a knowledge cutoff date and genuinely doesn't know what happened after it
Legal or regulatory claimsDrug approvals, laws, clinical guidelines — the stakes are high if something is off
Quotes attributed to real peopleAI will confidently put words in real people's mouths
Niche or technical detailsThe less common the topic, the more likely the model is filling in gaps

On the lower-risk end: general explanations of well-known concepts, summaries of broadly documented events, and things that are just common knowledge.

3
Ask AI to show its work
A better prompt can surface uncertainty before you go looking for it

Before you go off searching for sources, try asking the AI to back up what it said. It won't catch every error, but it can quickly reveal whether the model has any real grounding for a claim or is simply filling space.

Vague prompt — opens the door to fabrication

"What are the statistics on remote work productivity?"

More specific prompt — invites honesty

"What are the statistics on remote work productivity? For each statistic you mention, tell me the source and year. If you're not confident in a specific number, say so."

When asked to cite its sources, a good AI will often acknowledge uncertainty directly: "I'm not confident in that figure" or "I don't have a reliable source for this." That kind of hedging is actually useful information. What you want to watch out for is the opposite: a very specific citation delivered with total confidence, which is usually a sign something needs to be verified.

4
Verify with primary sources
Go to the original source. Another AI won't help here.

When something needs verifying, go straight to a primary source. One trap people fall into is asking a different AI tool to check the first one. That likely won't work because both models can draw from similar training data and produce the same fabricated result.

A few reliable places to look, depending on what you're checking:

Medical / clinical → PubMed Clinical trials → ClinicalTrials.gov Drug approvals → FDA.gov Health statistics → CDC, WHO, NIH Scientific papers → Google Scholar Breaking news → Reuters, AP News Legal & regulatory → .gov sites
What not to do

Paste an AI-generated citation into ChatGPT or Perplexity and ask if it's real. There's a good chance the second AI will simply confirm what the first one made up.

What actually works

Copy the citation directly into PubMed or Google Scholar and search for the paper. If nothing comes up, the AI invented it.

5
Watch for confident-sounding vagueness
Sounding confident and being accurate are two different things

Because AI is trained to produce fluent, coherent text, it sounds confident even when it's wrong. One of the trickier patterns to catch is what you might call "confident hedging," language that reads as measured and thoughtful while actually offering no evidence at all.

Sounds balanced but has no grounding

"While exact figures vary by source, most research suggests that approximately 60–70% of patients with this condition experience symptom improvement within 6 months."

What those phrases are really signaling

Phrases like "most research suggests," "studies generally show," and "it is widely accepted" aren't citations. They're placeholders. Treat them as a prompt to dig deeper, not as reassurance.

When something reads as perfectly reasonable and you feel inclined to just trust it, that's a good moment to pause. Plausibility isn't the same as accuracy, and AI output tends to be very good at sounding plausible.
6
Build a personal verification habit
A simple way to decide how much checking is enough

Realistically, the level of scrutiny you apply should match what's at stake. Here's a rough framework that's easy to keep in mind:

Low stakes

Brainstorming, drafting, organizing your own thinking — a quick read-through is probably fine

Medium stakes

Anything going to a team or being shared more broadly — check statistics and citations before it goes out

High stakes

Patient care, legal, regulatory, or public-facing work — every factual claim should be traced back to a primary source

A frame that tends to stick: think of AI output like a first draft from a smart, well-read colleague who sometimes misremembers their sources. You'd still read it carefully before putting your name on it.
